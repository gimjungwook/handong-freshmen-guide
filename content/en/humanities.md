# Humanities & Social Science Freshman Guide

> A course registration strategy for freshmen interested in Business, Economics, Law, International Relations, Psychology, Communication, and Social Welfare
> Main guide: [[Spring 2026 Freshman Registration Guide]]

## 🎯 1. Who Is This Guide For?

This guide is for freshmen considering any of the following departments and majors:

- **School of Management and Economics**: Business Administration, Economics, Accounting, Marketing
- **School of International Language and Culture**: International Area Studies, International Relations
- **School of Law**: Law
- **School of Communication Arts**: Media Communication, Performing & Visual Arts
- **School of Counseling Psychology and Social Welfare**: Counseling Psychology, Social Welfare
- **Global Leadership School (GLS)**: International Studies

Here's what makes the humanities and social sciences special: it's all about **broad exploration and the ability to write well**. Unlike STEM, where the path follows a pretty linear trajectory (Math → Physics → Major courses), the humanities and social sciences reward a process of encountering diverse disciplines and gradually narrowing your interests. So use your freshman year to take several intro courses and test whether a field *actually* fits you — not just whether it sounds cool on paper.

### 🌍 Critical Info for International Students

Not all majors at Handong can be completed entirely in English — and **this is something you absolutely need to understand before choosing your path.** Some departments offer their courses almost exclusively in Korean, which makes graduation really difficult if you're not fluent.

**Majors with enough English-taught courses for graduation (recommended for international students):**
- **School of Management and Economics**
- **School of Languages and Cultures**
- **School of Counseling Psychology and Social Welfare** (partially)
- **Global Leadership School (GLS)**

**Majors without enough English-taught courses (Korean proficiency required):**
- **School of Law**
- **School of Communication Arts** (mostly taught in Korean)

If you're not confident in your Korean yet, it's wise to pick from the recommended majors above. Or consider **Student-Designed Convergence Studies** or **Global Convergence Studies**, which we'll introduce below.

### 🧩 Student-Designed Convergence Studies — Design Your Own Major

This is genuinely one of the coolest programs at Handong. Offered through the Creative Convergence Education Center (창의융합교육원), it lets you **combine 3 or more fields to design your own interdisciplinary major**. For example, you could combine International Relations + Economics + Data Analysis to create a custom major like "Global Policy Analysis." It operates as a second major (33 credits) and requires a GPA of 3.0 or higher to apply. Applications are announced via HISNet at the beginning of each semester.

There's also **Global Convergence Studies** (글로벌융합전공), which lets you build a major primarily from English-taught courses across the university. This is an especially excellent option for international students. Because you can freely combine English-taught courses from across departments, you're not limited by any single department's Korean-language course ratios. If no department perfectly matches your interests, or if you need to make sure all your courses are in English, this gives you the flexibility to build exactly the path that works for you.

## 📚 2. Recommended Courses for Freshmen

### 🏷️ How to Read Course Codes

The **first digit in the course code tells you the year level**. `MEC10001` is a first-year course, while `MEC30001` is a third-year course. As a freshman, focus on courses with `1` in the code. Taking third- or fourth-year courses without the proper foundation isn't just a waste of time — it can seriously hurt your GPA.

Also, **don't jump into advanced major courses before you've declared your major.** Take intro courses first to get a real taste of the field, and only move to major courses once you're confident in your choice. That's the proper order, and it'll save you from regret.

### 🔍 Introductory Courses for Major Exploration

The key during your freshman year is to **take at least two intro courses**. Each department's intro course is basically a showcase — it shows you what the field covers and where it can take you career-wise. The feeling of "I think this might suit me" can be totally different from how you actually feel after studying it for a semester, so make sure to take the course before you commit!

#### 🔍 Introduction to Economics (MEC10001)

Economics is the study of how societies allocate resources. You'll learn systematic ways to think about questions like "Why do prices rise?" and "What happens to employment when the minimum wage goes up?" Concepts like supply and demand, market equilibrium, and GDP might feel abstract at first, but they become a powerful lens for understanding the news and public policy.

#### 🔍 Introduction to Business Administration (MEC10002)

Business administration is all about how companies work. It gives you a bird's-eye view of every major business function — marketing, finance, human resources, and strategy. It might look similar to economics, but the perspective is completely different. Economics asks "how does society allocate resources as a whole?" while business asks "how does a single company survive and grow?" We'd recommend trying both and seeing which one clicks for you.

### 📊 Statistics — The Language of Social Science

If you think being in the humanities means you can escape numbers... think again! Psychological research is impossible without statistical analysis, economics is fundamentally about interpreting data, and sociology relies on survey analysis. Statistics is the **common language** of the social sciences, so building a foundation now gives you a massive advantage when your major courses hit in second year and beyond.

## 💻 3. ICT Programming — Required Even for Humanities Students

The thought "I'm a humanities student, so I don't need to code" is **outdated and just plain wrong**. Handong requires all students to complete 7 credits of ICT convergence fundamentals, and humanities students are no exception. Data analysis is central to business, digital literacy is essential in media — this is the reality of the world we live in.

If it feels overwhelming, start with **Introduction to Software** (2 credits). It introduces basic programming concepts in a lightweight, approachable way. If you're up for more of a challenge, we'd recommend **Python Programming** (3 credits). Python is easy to learn yet incredibly practical for data analysis, automation, web scraping, and tons of other applications relevant to the humanities and social sciences.

Here's the thing — if you learn both statistics and Python, you become a "humanities/social science major who can handle data." That's a *powerful* differentiator in the job market. Since you have to take these courses anyway, don't just go through the motions — **actually learn the material.** Your future self will thank you.

## ⚠️ 5. Common Mistakes Humanities & Social Science Students Make

### ❌ "Intro courses are easy, so I can take them later"

Sure, intro courses are *relatively* easier. But **your freshman year is the only time you really have to explore.** By sophomore year, you've got to declare your major, and major courses start stacking up. The window for sampling different fields through intro courses is right now — this semester. If you realize in your second year that "this major wasn't for me after all," you've already lost a year. Explore now while you can!

### ❌ "Coding is for STEM people, so I'll just get through it"

Those ICT 7 credits are a graduation requirement. If you treat them with a "just get through it" attitude, you'll get bad grades — and those grades hit your GPA just like any other course. Since you have to take them anyway, **actually learn the material.** In the digital age, a humanities graduate who understands coding has a seriously strong competitive edge. Especially in business, marketing, and social sciences, data analysis skills aren't just nice to have — they're essential.

### ❌ "I'll just pick the easiest electives for good grades"

GPA management is important, but general education courses are **your only chance to broaden your horizons.** If you only pick comfortable, easy courses, you'll graduate without much to show for it. Handong's open major system was designed so you'd explore on your own. Don't waste that opportunity by playing it safe!

### ❌ "Taking advanced major courses before declaring a major"

Some students take third- or fourth-year major courses out of curiosity before they've even declared. Always check the first digit of the course code. A course like `MEC30001` is third-year level. Taking advanced courses without the foundation means you won't understand the material and you're likely to get rough grades. Follow the order: Introduction → Foundational → Advanced. It exists for a reason.

### ❌ "Not checking the English course ratio" (International students)

As we've emphasized, some majors simply don't have enough English-taught courses. Before deciding on a major, **you must check the ratio of English-taught courses in that department.** You might find that intro courses were available in English during freshman year, but starting from sophomore year, everything switches to Korean — at which point graduation becomes extremely difficult. Do your homework on this early!

## 🔭 6. Preview of Second Semester

Based on what you discover in your first semester, here's how you might plan semester two:

**Narrow your major direction:** If you took two or more intro courses in the first semester, take a foundational course in whichever field excited you most. For example, if Introduction to Economics was engaging, level up to Microeconomics or Macroeconomics.

**Fill in missed introductions:** If there was an intro course in another field that didn't fit your first-semester schedule, explore it now. If you haven't locked in your major choice, it's perfectly fine to keep exploring.

**Take Statistics:** If you skipped Statistics in the first semester, make sure to take it in the second. It's an essential tool for social science research and a skill you'll need right away in your major courses.

**Data and Statistical Processing:** If you knocked out Statistics in the first semester, take Data and Statistical Processing in the second to build up your practical skills.

**Logic and Critical Thinking:** If you took a writing course in the first semester, add logical thinking training in the second. Writing + Logical Thinking = the most powerful combo for producing reports and papers in the humanities and social sciences.

**Explore Student-Designed Convergence Studies:** If none of the existing departments felt quite right, you can design your own major through the Creative Convergence Education Center's Student-Designed Convergence Studies program. Applications open each semester via HISNet — it's a fantastic option that too few students know about!
