# Humanities & Social Science Freshman Guide

> A course registration strategy for freshmen interested in Business, Economics, Law, International Relations, Psychology, Communication, and Social Welfare
> Main guide: [[Spring 2026 Freshman Registration Guide]]

## 🎯 1. Who Is This Guide For?

This guide is written for freshmen considering the following departments and majors:

- **School of Management & Economics**: Business Administration, Economics, Accounting, Marketing
- **School of International Studies & English**: International Relations, English, Chinese
- **School of Law**: Law
- **School of Communication Arts**: Media, Journalism, Content Creation
- **School of Counseling, Psychology, & Social Welfare**: Psychology, Counseling, Social Welfare
- **School of Global Leadership (GLS)**: International Studies

The essence of the humanities and social sciences is **broad exploration and the ability to write well**. Unlike STEM fields, where the path follows a linear trajectory (Math → Physics → Major courses), the humanities and social sciences reward a process of encountering diverse disciplines and gradually narrowing your interests. Use your freshman year to take several introductory courses and test whether a field truly fits you.

### 🌍 Critical Information for International Students

Not all majors at Handong can be completed entirely in English — **this is something you absolutely must understand before choosing your path.** Some departments offer their courses almost exclusively in Korean, making graduation extremely difficult for students who are not fluent in Korean.

**Majors with sufficient English-taught courses for graduation (recommended for international students):**
- **School of Management & Economics**
- **School of International Studies & English**
- **School of Counseling, Psychology, & Social Welfare** (partially)
- **School of Global Leadership (GLS)**

**Majors with insufficient English-taught courses (Korean proficiency required):**
- **School of Law**
- **School of Communication Arts** (mostly taught in Korean)

If you are not confident in your Korean, it is wise to choose from the recommended majors listed above. Alternatively, strongly consider **GCS (Global Convergence Studies)**, which is introduced below.

### 🧩 GCS (Global Convergence Studies) — Design Your Own Major

GCS is a program that allows you to **design your own interdisciplinary major** without being confined to a single department. For example, you could combine International Relations + Economics + Data Analysis to create a custom major like "Global Policy Analysis."

To begin the GCS track, you must first take the course **"Vision, Work, and Calling"** (비전, 일, 소명). In this course, you explore your personal vision and go through the process of designing a curriculum that aligns with it.

GCS is an especially excellent option for international students. Because you can freely combine courses that are offered in English from across the university, you are not constrained by the Korean-language course ratios of any particular department. If no single department perfectly matches your interests, or if you need to ensure all your courses are in English, GCS gives you the flexibility to build a path that works.

## 📚 2. Recommended Courses for Freshmen

### 🏷️ How to Read Course Codes

The **first digit in the course code indicates the year level**. `MEC10001` is a first-year course, while `MEC30001` is a third-year course. As a freshman, focus on courses with `1` in the code. Taking third- or fourth-year courses without the proper foundation is not only a waste of time — it can be devastating to your GPA.

Also, **do not take advanced major courses before you have declared your major.** Take introductory courses first to get a taste of the field, and only move to major courses once you are confident in your choice. That is the proper order.

If mathematics is the weapon of STEM students, **writing** is the weapon of humanities and social science students. Reports, theses, proposals, policy briefs — everything begins and ends with writing. Handong offers several writing and presentation courses, so choose the one that best fits your needs.

### 🔍 Introductory Courses for Major Exploration

The key during your freshman year is to **take at least two introductory courses**. Each department's intro course acts as a showcase — showing you what the field covers and what careers it leads to. The feeling of "I think this field might suit me" can be completely different from how you actually feel after studying it, so make sure to take the course before you decide.

#### 🔍 Introduction to Economics (MEC10001)

Economics is the study of how societies allocate resources. You will learn systematic approaches to questions like "Why do prices rise?" and "What effect does raising the minimum wage have on employment?" Concepts like supply and demand, market equilibrium, and GDP might feel abstract at first, but they become a powerful lens for understanding the news and public policy.

**Section 01 is taught 100% in English and is ideal for international students.** If you prefer Korean instruction, choose Section 02 (Professor Ahn Jinwon).

#### 🔍 Introduction to Business Administration (MEC10002)

Business administration is about how companies are run. It offers a bird's-eye view of all business functions — marketing, finance, human resources, and strategy. It may look similar to economics, but the perspective is completely different. While economics examines "how society allocates resources as a whole," business asks "how a single firm survives and grows." We recommend trying both and choosing the one that resonates with you.

### 📊 Statistics — The Language of Social Science

If you think being in the humanities means you can avoid numbers, think again. Psychological research is impossible without statistical analysis, economics is fundamentally about interpreting data, and sociology relies on survey analysis with statistics. Statistics is the **common language** of the social sciences, so building a foundation in your freshman year gives you an enormous advantage when you reach your major courses in second year and beyond.

## 💻 3. ICT Programming — Required Even for Humanities Students

The thought "I'm a humanities student, so I don't need to code" is **outdated and wrong**. Handong requires all students to complete 7 credits of ICT convergence fundamentals, and humanities students are no exception. Data analysis is central to business, and digital literacy is essential in media — this is the reality of our era.

If it feels overwhelming, start with **Introduction to Software** (2 credits). It introduces basic programming concepts in a lightweight way. If you want more of a challenge, we recommend **Python Programming** (3 credits). Python is easy to learn yet practically useful for data analysis, automation, web scraping, and other applications relevant to the humanities and social sciences.

If you learn both statistics and Python, you become a "humanities/social science major who can handle data" — a powerful differentiator in the job market. Since you have to take these courses anyway, do not just go through the motions — **learn them properly.**

## ⚠️ 5. Common Mistakes Humanities & Social Science Students Make

### ❌ "Intro courses are easy, so I can take them later"

It is true that introductory courses are relatively easier. But **your freshman year is the only time you have to explore.** By sophomore year, you must declare your major, and major courses start piling up. The window for sampling different fields through intro courses is right now. If you realize in your second year that "this major was not for me after all," you have already wasted a year. Explore diligently now.

### ❌ "Coding is for STEM people, so I'll just get through it"

ICT 7 credits are a graduation requirement. If you approach them with a "just get through it" attitude, you will earn poor grades, and those grades will be reflected directly in your GPA. Since you have to take these courses anyway, **learn them properly.** In the digital age, a humanities graduate who understands coding has a markedly stronger competitive edge in the job market. Especially in business, marketing, and social sciences, data analysis capability is no longer optional — it is essential.

### ❌ "I'll just pick the easiest electives for good grades"

GPA management matters, but general education courses are **your only chance to broaden your horizons.** If you only pick comfortable courses, you will graduate with nothing to show for it. Handong's open major system was designed so that you would explore on your own. Do not waste that opportunity.

### ❌ "Taking advanced major courses before declaring a major"

Some students take third- or fourth-year major courses out of sheer curiosity before they have even declared their major. Always check the first digit of the course code. A course like `MEC30001` is third-year level. Taking advanced courses without the proper foundation means you will not understand the material, and you are likely to receive poor grades. Follow the order: Introduction → Foundational → Advanced.

### ❌ "Not checking the English course ratio" (International students)

As emphasized earlier, some majors have far too few English-taught courses. Before deciding on a major, **you must check the ratio of English-taught courses in that department.** You may find that introductory courses were available in English during your freshman year, but starting from sophomore year, all major courses are in Korean — at which point graduation becomes extremely difficult.

## 🔭 6. Preview of Second Semester

Based on your first-semester exploration, here is how you might plan your second semester:

**Narrow your major direction:** If you took two or more introductory courses in the first semester, take a foundational course in your second semester in whichever field interested you most. For example, if Introduction to Economics was engaging, move into Microeconomics or Macroeconomics.

**Fill in missed introductions:** If there was an introductory course in another field that did not fit your first-semester schedule, explore it in the second semester. If you have not yet finalized your major choice, it is perfectly right to continue exploring.

**Take Statistics:** If you did not take Statistics in the first semester, make sure to take it in the second. It is an essential tool for social science research and a skill you will need immediately in your major courses.

**Data and Statistical Processing:** If you completed Statistics in the first semester, take Data and Statistical Processing in the second to supplement your practical skills.

**Logic and Critical Thinking:** If you took a writing course in the first semester, add logical thinking training in the second. Writing + Logical Thinking = the most powerful combination for producing reports and papers in the humanities and social sciences.

**Explore GCS:** If none of the existing departments felt like the right fit, you can begin designing your own major through GCS by taking the "Vision, Work, and Calling" course.
