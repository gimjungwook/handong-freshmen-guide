# Registration Strategy

> Knowing what courses to take is necessary, but it is not enough. You also need to know *when* things happen, *how* to execute on registration day, and *what mistakes* to avoid. This page covers the complete timeline, a step-by-step registration day battle plan, common freshman errors, and contact information for when you need help.

### Pre-registration Rules (CRITICAL — Read This Carefully)

Pre-registration works like an online shopping cart. You place the courses you want into your cart, but **adding a course to your cart does not guarantee enrollment**. Here is how it actually works:

- **If the number of applicants is less than or equal to the course capacity** → Everyone who applied is **automatically confirmed**. No further action needed.
- **If the number of applicants exceeds the course capacity** → **Nobody is confirmed**. The course resets, and everyone must compete again during the main registration on a first-come-first-served basis.

This is a crucial distinction. Many freshmen assume that pre-registration guarantees their spot and then panic on main registration day when they discover their courses were not confirmed.

**After pre-registration ends, you MUST log into HISNet to check which courses were confirmed.** Any course that was not confirmed must be re-registered during the main registration period. Prepare accordingly.

### 📊 Use Pre-registration Numbers to Prioritize

After pre-registration closes, you can **check the number of pre-registration applicants for each course on HISNet**. Compare this number to the course capacity to determine your main registration priority:

- **High applicant-to-capacity ratio** → Fierce competition → Register this course **first** on main registration day
- **Ratio at or below 1.0** → Already auto-confirmed in pre-registration → **No action needed**

For example, if General Biology has 40 seats and 120 pre-registrants (ratio: 3.0), that's extreme competition. But if Chapel has 200 seats and 150 pre-registrants, it's already confirmed — ignore it. Planning your registration order based on these ratios helps you stay calm and systematic on the day.

## ⚔️ Registration Day Strategy

Registration day is intense. Having a clear battle plan makes the difference between a successful semester and a chaotic one. Follow this sequence:

### Before Registration Day

1. **Prepare at least 3 timetable drafts.** Your preferred sections may fill instantly. Without backup plans (Plan B, Plan C), you will panic in the moment and make poor decisions.

2. **Write course codes on paper and tape them next to your monitor.** Searching for course names in the registration system wastes precious seconds. Having codes like `GEK20058` and section numbers ready for instant entry is significantly faster.

3. **Test your login at [sugang.handong.edu](https://sugang.handong.edu) beforehand.** Your ID and password are the same as HISNet. If you forget your password on registration day, you are finished.

### Registration Sequence (In Order of Priority)

1. **Log into [sugang.handong.edu](https://sugang.handong.edu) 5 minutes before the start time.** Have the page loaded and ready.

2. **Register Chapel + Community Leadership Training first.** These are quick and non-competitive — get them out of the way immediately.

3. **Register your most competitive courses next.** General Biology English section (Section 02) is EXTREMELY competitive. English sections of popular courses (Bible, Worldview) fill fast. Hit these immediately.

4. **Register English courses (EF/EC/ERC/EAP).** Do not delay — professors are enforcing strict capacity limits now.

5. **Register major exploration courses** (introductory courses in your areas of interest).

6. **If any course is full, switch to your backup timetable IMMEDIATELY.** Do not waste time refreshing and hoping — move to Plan B.

7. **Final check:** Verify no time conflicts, confirm total credits are in your target range (15.5-22 credits).

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes

These mistakes happen every single semester. Learn from the students who came before you.

### Not Registering Chapel

This is the number one freshman mistake. You attend Chapel every Wednesday for an entire semester. You scan the QR code faithfully. At the end of the semester, you check your transcript — Chapel is not there. You never registered it in the system. An entire semester of attendance, wasted. **Register Chapel in the course registration system.**

### Taking 3xxx/4xxx Courses as a Freshman

The course registration system may allow you to enroll, but that does not mean you should. Upper-level courses assume prerequisite knowledge that you do not yet have. You will struggle, your grade will suffer, and you will have wasted a course slot that could have gone to a foundational subject.

### Not Checking English/Korean Per Section

Professor Joshua Kim teaches Understanding the Bible. Section 07 is 100% English. Sections 08 and 09 are 0% English (Korean). Same professor, completely different language. **Always check the specific section's language percentage.**

### Guessing on the Korean Placement Test

As discussed in the tips page, guessing your way into a higher Korean level creates more problems than it solves. You end up in a class beyond your ability and must fill the lower-level credit gaps with other courses. Answer honestly and start at your true level.

### Taking Too Few Credits

Registering for only 14-15 credits feels safe but leaves you with no room for error. If you need to drop a course after the first week, you may fall below the 15.5-credit minimum (relevant for scholarship students) or below a healthy credit load. **Start with 18-20 credits and drop down as needed.** Adding courses later is nearly impossible for popular ones.

### Not Having a Backup Schedule

If your Plan A fails — and for competitive courses, it often does — you need Plan B and Plan C ready before you sit down on registration day. Students without backups waste critical minutes during the main registration period trying to figure out alternatives in real-time.

### Delaying English Courses

"I will take my English course next semester." This is a dangerous gamble. Capacity limits are strict, and sections fill quickly. Take your assigned English level immediately. Waiting gains you nothing and risks being shut out for an entire semester.
