# Registration Strategy

> Knowing what courses to take is necessary, but it's not enough. You also need to know *when* things happen, *how* to execute on registration day, and *what mistakes* to avoid. This page covers the complete timeline, a step-by-step registration day battle plan, common freshman errors, and contact info for when you need help. Let's get you prepared!

### Pre-registration Rules (CRITICAL — Please Read This Carefully)

Pre-registration works like an online shopping cart. You place courses you want into your cart, but — and this is the key part — **adding a course to your cart doesn't guarantee enrollment**. Here's how it actually works:

- **If the number of applicants is less than or equal to the course capacity** → Everyone who applied is **automatically confirmed**. No action needed — you're in!
- **If the number of applicants exceeds the course capacity** → **Nobody is confirmed**. The course resets, and everyone has to compete again during main registration on a first-come-first-served basis.

This is a *huge* distinction that trips up freshmen every year. Many students assume pre-registration guarantees their spot and then panic on main registration day when they discover their courses weren't confirmed.

**After pre-registration ends, you MUST log into HISNet to check which courses were confirmed.** Any course that wasn't confirmed needs to be re-registered during main registration. Be prepared!

### 📊 Use Pre-registration Numbers to Prioritize

Here's a pro tip — after pre-registration closes, you can **check the number of pre-registration applicants for each course on HISNet**. Compare this number to the course capacity to figure out your main registration priority:

- **High applicant-to-capacity ratio** → Fierce competition → Register this course **first** on main registration day
- **Ratio at or below 1.0** → Already auto-confirmed in pre-registration → **You're good, no action needed**

For example, if General Biology has 40 seats and 120 pre-registrants (ratio: 3.0), that's extreme competition. But if Chapel has 200 seats and 150 pre-registrants, it's already confirmed — don't waste time on it. Planning your registration order based on these ratios keeps you calm and systematic when the clock is ticking.

## ⚔️ Registration Day Strategy

Registration day is intense — there's no sugarcoating it. But having a clear battle plan makes the difference between a great semester and a chaotic one. Here's your game plan:

### Before Registration Day

1. **Prepare at least 3 timetable drafts.** Your preferred sections may fill up in seconds. Without backup plans (Plan B, Plan C), you'll panic in the moment and make bad decisions. Don't wing it!

2. **Write course codes on paper and tape them next to your monitor.** Searching for course names in the registration system wastes precious seconds. Having codes like `GEK20058` and section numbers ready for instant entry is *so* much faster.

3. **Test your login at [sugang.handong.edu](https://sugang.handong.edu) beforehand.** Your ID and password are the same as HISNet. If you forget your password on registration day, it's game over.

### Registration Sequence (In Order of Priority)

1. **Log into [sugang.handong.edu](https://sugang.handong.edu) 5 minutes before the start time.** Have the page loaded and ready to go.

2. **Register Chapel + Community Leadership Training first.** These are quick and non-competitive — knock 'em out immediately.

3. **Register your most competitive courses next.** General Biology English section (Section 02) is EXTREMELY competitive. English sections of popular courses (Bible, Worldview) fill fast. Hit these right away.

4. **Register English courses (EF/EC/ERC/EAP).** Don't delay — professors are enforcing strict capacity limits now.

5. **Register major exploration courses** (introductory courses in your areas of interest).

6. **If any course is full, switch to your backup timetable IMMEDIATELY.** Don't waste time refreshing and hoping — that ship has sailed. Move to Plan B.

7. **Final check:** Verify no time conflicts, confirm total credits are appropriate (max 22 credits for freshmen).

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes

These mistakes happen every single semester. Learn from the students who came before you — they wish someone had told them this stuff.

### Not Registering Chapel

This is the #1 freshman mistake and it hurts every time. You attend Chapel every Wednesday for an entire semester. You scan the QR code faithfully. At the end of the semester, you check your transcript — Chapel isn't there. You never registered it in the system. An entire semester of attendance, completely wasted. **Please, please register Chapel in the course registration system.**

### Taking 3xxx/4xxx Courses as a Freshman

The registration system might let you enroll, but that doesn't mean you should. Upper-level courses assume prerequisite knowledge you don't have yet. You'll struggle, your grade will tank, and you'll have wasted a course slot that could've gone to a foundational subject. Resist the temptation!

### Not Checking English/Korean Per Section

Professor Joshua Kim teaches Understanding the Bible. Section 07 is 100% English. Sections 08 and 09 are 0% English (Korean). Same professor, completely different language. **Always check the specific section's language percentage.** This one's easy to mess up, so double-check!

### Guessing on the Korean Placement Test

As we mentioned in the tips page, guessing your way into a higher Korean level creates way more problems than it solves. You'll end up in a class beyond your ability and have to fill the lower-level credit gaps with other courses. Answer honestly and start at your true level — it's the smart move.

### Taking Too Few Credits

Registering for only 14-15 credits feels safe but leaves you with zero room for error. If you need to drop a course after the first week, you might fall below the 15.5-credit minimum (relevant for scholarship students) or below a healthy credit load. **Start with 18-20 credits and drop down as needed.** Adding popular courses later is nearly impossible — don't put yourself in that position.

### Not Having a Backup Schedule

If your Plan A fails — and for competitive courses, it often does — you need Plan B and Plan C ready *before* you sit down on registration day. Students without backups waste critical minutes during main registration trying to figure out alternatives on the fly. Don't be that person.

### Delaying English Courses

"I'll take my English course next semester." Nope — this is a dangerous gamble. Capacity limits are strict, and sections fill quickly. Take your assigned English level right away. Waiting gains you nothing and risks being shut out for an entire semester.
