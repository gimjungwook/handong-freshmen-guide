# Exploratory Freshman Guide

> A course registration strategy for freshmen who haven't decided on a major yet, or who want to sample different fields before committing.
> Main guide: [[Spring 2026 Freshman Registration Guide]]

---

## 🎯 1. Who This Guide Is For

Not having chosen a major yet? **That's absolutely nothing to be embarrassed about.** In fact, you're in the best possible position to take full advantage of Handong's open-major system!

Here's something we see every year: many students lock in a major before they even arrive on campus — "Engineering has good job prospects," or "My parents want me to study business." Then sophomore year rolls around and the regret hits: "This wasn't the right path for me." Choosing a major shapes at least the next three years and potentially your whole career. Taking one semester to explore before deciding isn't late at all — it's actually the smartest thing you can do.

Handong also has this unique option called **Student-Designed Convergence Studies** (학생설계융합전공). This program, operated by the Creative Convergence Education Center, lets you combine 3 or more majors or fields (programs, modules, etc.), including at least 2 actual majors, to design your own custom major. It operates exclusively as a second major (33 credits), so you must have a separate primary major, and requires a GPA of 3.0+. Applications open each semester via HISNet, so keep this path in mind while you're exploring!

This guide shows you course combinations that let you **sample as many fields as possible while making sure nothing goes to waste, no matter what major you end up choosing.** Pay attention to the first digit of each course code: a 1 means it's an introductory course for first-year students. When you haven't settled on a major yet, taking upper-level courses (codes starting with 3 or 4) is risky — **sticking to intro-level courses is the key strategy.**

## 🧭 2. The Exploration Strategy: The 3+2 Principle

To get the most out of your exploration in a single semester, we recommend combining **3 introductory courses from different fields + 2 universal foundation courses.**

### 💡 Why Three Fields?

Here's an analogy that really clicks. When you're searching for a great restaurant, visiting just one place leaves you thinking, "It was okay, I guess." Visiting two lets you say, "I think B was better than A" — basically just elimination. But when you visit three, something different happens: you can make an **active discovery** — "This place is genuinely amazing!"

Choosing a major works the exact same way.

- With 1 course, you've got no basis for comparison — real judgment is impossible
- With 2, you're stuck in passive elimination: "This one seems slightly better than that one"
- **With 3, you unlock active discovery: "This field genuinely excites me!"**

And here's the thing — it's incredibly common for students to discover unexpected passion in a field they thought they'd have zero interest in. That's why we strongly recommend picking at least **1 of your 3 from a field that feels deliberately unfamiliar.** Surprise yourself!

### 🧱 What Are the 2 Foundation Courses?

These are two courses that'll serve you well regardless of what major you ultimately choose.

**First: programming.** Whether it's Introduction to Software or Introduction to Front-End Development, coding experience is now essential across every discipline. Even if you major in business, you'll need programming for data analysis. Even in psychology, you'll need to read code to process experimental data. It's everywhere.

**Second: a quantitative foundation.** If you're leaning toward STEM, take Calculus 1. If you're leaning toward humanities or social sciences, take Introduction to Statistics. If you genuinely have no idea? Statistics is the more versatile choice — it's used in business, psychology, social science, and natural science alike.

### ✨ The Ideal First Semester

Based on our experience helping freshmen, the ideal first semester for an exploratory student looks like this:

| Category | Course | Notes |

The strategy is simple: place required courses first, then fill remaining slots with exploration courses. And here's one crucial tip: **register for more credits than you think you need and drop during the first week, rather than playing it safe.** You can register for up to 18 credits, so load up on courses that interest you, attend the first week in person, and then adjust during the course correction period (수강정정). Starting with exactly 15 credits and nothing more leaves you zero room to maneuver. Don't box yourself in!

## 🍽️ 3. Field-by-Field Tasting Menu

Pick **3 fields that catch your eye** from the options below. Every course listed is based on actual Spring 2026 data, and all are introductory-level (first digit of course code = 1). The one exception is Introduction to Education (GEK20042), which has a 2nd-year course code but can be taken without prerequisites. Unless you're already confident about a particular major, don't touch the advanced courses.

### ⚙️ Engineering & Technology Sampler

Most intro courses here are 2 credits, making them low-commitment. Think of them as a small investment to answer one big question: "Is engineering right for me?"

**Introduction to Software (GCS10001)** — 2 credits, 6 sections offered

A course that teaches foundational programming concepts and logical thinking. Never coded before? That's totally fine — this course is literally designed for people starting from zero. You'll begin with the basics (variables, conditionals, loops) and build simple programs. It's your first step in figuring out whether programming fits your brain, and it builds essential digital-era skills no matter what you end up majoring in.

**Introduction to AI, Computer & Electronic Engineering (ECE10006)** — 2 credits, Mon periods 6–7

An intro course that lets you sample three fields — AI, computer science, and electronic engineering — all in one course. It gives a broad overview of what problems each field tackles and what practitioners actually do. The emphasis is on breadth, not depth, so it's perfect for students thinking, "I have absolutely no clue what engineering even is." No judgment — that's exactly who this course is for!

**Introduction to Front-End Development (GCS10081)** — 2 credits, 6 sections offered

A hands-on course where you build actual websites. You'll learn HTML, CSS, and JavaScript and see visible results right away — that's the magic of this course. While Intro to Software focuses on logical thinking, Front-End focuses on **the joy of building things.** If you've got even a passing interest in design, this course lets you experience where technology and creativity meet.

**Introduction to Physics (GEK10090)** — 3 credits, Tue period 2 & Fri period 2 / Tue period 3 & Fri period 3

Core physics concepts at a level that's accessible to non-majors. It's broader but shallower than Physics 1, making it ideal if you're thinking, "I want to get what physics is about, but I'm not going to major in it." It builds great engineering intuition, and even if you later choose a STEM major, it's solid prep.

### 📖 Humanities & Social Sciences Sampler

Most courses here are 3 credits, and many offer sections taught entirely in English. If English-medium instruction feels daunting, just pick a Korean-medium section — no pressure!

**Introduction to Business Administration (MEC10002)** — 3 credits, 3 sections (**Section 01: 100% English**)

A course about how businesses actually work. In one semester, you'll survey all the major domains: marketing, finance, human resources, and strategy. Whether you're dreaming of a startup or a corporate career, understanding how organizations operate is useful no matter what path you take. If there's even a tiny chance you might start a business after graduation, seriously consider taking this one.

**Introduction to Economics (MEC10001)** — 3 credits, 2 sections (**Section 01: 100% English**)

The study of how money and resources flow through the world. You'll learn about supply and demand, market principles, and the effects of government policy. After this course, you'll actually understand why the news keeps talking about interest rate hikes and exchange rates — and how they affect your daily life. Because economics involves mathematical thinking, it also makes a great bridge for STEM-oriented students who want to dip into the humanities side.

**Introduction to Psychology (CSW10003)** — 3 credits, 3 sections (**Section 02: 100% English**)

The scientific study of the human mind and behavior. It covers the big topics: perception, memory, learning, motivation, emotion, personality, and social behavior. If you're drawn to the question "Why do people do what they do?", this is your course. Here's a fun surprise — psychology uses a ton of statistics and experimental design, making it a unique field that satisfies both scientific and humanistic curiosity at the same time.

**Introduction to International Relations (ISE10052)** — 3 credits, 2 sections (**Section 01: 100% English**)

A course about how nations interact and the big global issues of our time. Topics include war and peace, trade, diplomacy, international organizations, and human rights. You don't have to want to be a diplomat to benefit from this — anyone curious about "how the world actually works" will come away with a much broader perspective.

**Introduction to Philosophy (GEK10030)** — 3 credits, 2 sections (**Section 01: 100% English**)

"Why do I exist?" "What is justice?" "How can we know the truth?" This course tackles life's most fundamental questions. It might seem impractical on the surface, but philosophical thinking is the foundation of logical writing, critical analysis, and ethical judgment — skills that matter in *every* field. Philosophy also connects deeply to AI ethics, law, and policy studies. Don't sleep on this one.

**Introduction to Sociology (GEK10040)** — 3 credits, Tue periods 2–3

A discipline that analyzes social structures and phenomena. It covers class, inequality, culture, institutions, and collective behavior, giving you a systematic framework for thinking about "Why does society work this way?" Great for students who want to look beyond individuals and understand the bigger picture.

**Introduction to Education (GEK20042)** — 3 credits, 4 sections offered

A course exploring the essence and principles of education. It's valuable not just for aspiring teachers, but for anyone interested in how people learn and grow. The applications of education studies are broader than you'd expect: corporate training, educational technology, content creation, and more.

### 🎨 Arts & Design Sampler

Arts and design courses tend to be practice-heavy, so the time investment can be bigger than other courses. But they offer an amazing opportunity to discover whether you've got artistic sensibility hiding in there.

**Drawing Fundamentals 1 (IID10004)** — 3 credits, Fri periods 2–3 / Fri periods 5–6 / Fri periods 7–8

A course in foundational drawing and visual expression. If you're thinking "I can't draw" — don't worry! This course trains observation skills and expression techniques, not innate talent. It's a fantastic starting point for anyone considering design, architecture, or media. Bonus: classes are only on Fridays, so it won't mess with your schedule on other days.

**Introduction to Spatial Studies (CUE10004)** — 3 credits, Tue period 1 & Fri period 1 / Mon period 4 & Thu period 4

A foundational course in architecture and spatial design. It explores how the spaces we live in are designed and how those spaces influence human behavior and emotion. If architecture or spatial environment studies sparks even a little curiosity, use this course to test the waters.

### 🏛️ History & Culture Sampler

History and culture courses broaden the lens through which you see the world. They're valuable as liberal arts regardless of your major, and they build both writing ability and critical thinking at the same time. Win-win.

**East Asian History and Culture (GEK10087)** — 3 credits, Mon period 3 & Thu period 3, **100% English**

A course covering the arc of East Asian civilization — China, Japan, and Korea. Since it's taught entirely in English, you'll sharpen your language skills too. Excellent background for international relations, cultural content, or diplomacy.

**Western History and Culture (GEK10088)** — 3 credits, Mon periods 2–3 / Mon periods 5–6

A journey through Western civilization — from ancient Greece and Rome through modern democratic revolutions and the Industrial Revolution. It's essential context for understanding why today's global political, economic, and social structures look the way they do. Both sections run as back-to-back classes on Mondays, which means you can concentrate your lectures and keep the rest of the week free.

**Understanding Korean Literature (GEK10033)** — 3 credits, Thu periods 6–7

Reading and analyzing Korean literary works across genres: novels, poetry, and essays. It develops literary sensitivity and critical reading skills, and provides a solid foundation if you're interested in content creation, media, or publishing.

**Globalization and Korean Popular Culture (GEK10104)** — 3 credits, Tue period 2 & Fri period 2, **100% English**

An academic look at how K-pop, K-drama, K-food, and other Korean pop culture went global. Taught entirely in English, it explores the relationship between cultural content and globalization. It's engaging, it's relevant, and it connects to media, marketing, international relations, and more. Honestly one of the most enjoyable courses on this list.

## ⚠️ 5. Things to Watch Out for While Exploring

### 🎯 Distinguish "Fun" from "Aptitude"

Just because the first class was enjoyable doesn't necessarily mean the field is your calling. Most professors make their opening lectures engaging and exciting on purpose. True aptitude shows up **when you hit the hard parts and still want to keep going.**

For example, if the first business class was fun during the case study but your interest vanished the moment accounting numbers appeared, you might've just been attracted to the glamorous surface. But if working with those numbers is tough yet you keep wondering "Why is this done this way?" — that persistent curiosity? That's the signal of genuine aptitude.

### 👥 Leverage Senior Student (섬김이) Interviews

The real info isn't in the course catalog — it's with the people who've been through it. We recommend meeting at least one upperclassman from each major you're considering and asking these three questions:

- "What's the hardest part of this major?"
- "What are you doing after graduation? / What do you plan to do?"
- "If you were a freshman again, would you still choose this major?"

These three questions will give you more insight than the entire course catalog combined. The answer to that third question, in particular, tends to be the most honest — and the most useful.

> Note: At Handong, 섬김이 (seomgimi) are student mentors assigned to freshman groups. They're awesome first contacts for this kind of candid conversation — don't hesitate to reach out!

### 🏷️ Check the Course Codes

The first digit of a course code indicates the target year level. Courses with 1 are for first-years; those with 3 or 4 are for juniors and seniors. Unless you've firmly decided on a major, **sticking to first-year intro courses is the safe play.** Upper-level courses often have prerequisites, and jumping in without background knowledge just results in a damaged GPA.

### ⏳ Postponing a Decision Is Still a Decision

Exploration is super important, but **you can't explore forever.** Spend the first semester gaining experience, then start setting a direction in the second semester. The perfect choice doesn't exist in this world. When you reach about 70% certainty, go for it. The remaining 30% fills itself in through action. Those who wait for the perfect moment end up never starting anything at all. So explore boldly, then commit courageously!

## 🔭 6. Second Semester Strategy

Depending on what your first-semester exploration reveals, three paths open up:

**If you've found a direction**, awesome! Switch to the relevant detailed guide. If you're leaning toward engineering, AI, or natural sciences, check out the [[STEM Freshman Guide]]. If you're drawn to business, economics, law, or international relations, head to the [[Humanities & Social Science Guide]]. Start taking core major foundation courses from your second semester onward.

**If you're still unsure**, that's okay too! Add 1–2 intro courses from fields you haven't tried yet, while also starting foundation courses in whichever field attracted you most during the first semester. For example, if economics was the most interesting, take one more economics foundation course in the second semester while adding an untried arts/design intro course.

**If two fields are pulling you equally**, take advantage of Handong's double major or interdisciplinary major systems — it's totally feasible! For instance, if both computer science and business excite you, you can pursue one as your primary major and the other as a double major. Or you can design a custom converged major as a second major through the Student-Designed Convergence Studies program that fuses 3 or more fields. Pretty cool options, right?
