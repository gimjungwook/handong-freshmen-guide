# Exploratory Freshman Guide

> A course registration strategy for freshmen who haven't decided on a major yet, or who want to sample diverse fields before committing.
> Main guide: [[Spring 2026 Freshman Registration Guide]]

---

## 🎯 1. Who This Guide Is For

Not having chosen a major is **nothing to be embarrassed about.** In fact, you are in the best possible position to take full advantage of Handong's open-major system.

Many students lock in a major before they even arrive on campus — "Engineering has good job prospects," or "My parents want me to study business." Then sophomore year rolls around and the regret sets in: "This wasn't the right path for me." Choosing a major is a decision that will shape at least the next three years, and potentially your entire career. Taking one semester to explore before deciding is not late at all — it is wise.

Handong also offers a unique option called **GCS (Global Convergence Studies)**. Rather than belonging to a traditional department, GCS lets you design your own custom major by converging multiple disciplines. If GCS interests you, note that you'll need to complete the "Vision Work and Calling" course, so keep this path in mind during your exploration.

This guide proposes course combinations that let you **sample as many fields as possible while ensuring nothing goes to waste, no matter what major you eventually choose.** Pay attention to the first digit of each course code: a 1 means it's an introductory course designed for first-year students. When you haven't settled on a major, taking upper-level courses (codes starting with 3 or 4) is risky — **sticking to introductory-level courses is the key strategy.**

## 🧭 2. The Exploration Strategy: The 3+2 Principle

To maximize the effectiveness of your exploration in a single semester, I recommend combining **3 introductory courses from different fields + 2 universal foundation courses.**

### 💡 Why Three Fields?

Let me offer an analogy. When searching for a great restaurant, visiting just one place leaves you thinking, "It was okay, I guess." Visiting two lets you say, "I think B was better than A" — a process of elimination at best. But when you visit three, something different happens: you can make an **active discovery** — "This place is genuinely amazing!"

Choosing a major works the same way.

- With 1 course, you have no basis for comparison — judgment itself is impossible
- With 2, you're stuck in passive elimination: "This one seems slightly better than that one"
- **With 3, you unlock active discovery: "This field is genuinely exciting to me!"**

It is remarkably common for students to discover unexpected aptitude in a field they assumed they'd have zero interest in. That's why I strongly recommend picking at least **1 of your 3 from a field that feels deliberately unfamiliar.**

### 🧱 What Are the 2 Foundation Courses?

These are two courses that will serve you well regardless of what major you ultimately choose.

**First: programming.** Whether it's Introduction to Software or Introduction to Front-End Development, coding experience is now an essential digital competency across every discipline. Even if you major in business, you'll need programming for data analysis. Even if you major in psychology, you'll need to read code to process experimental data.

**Second: a quantitative foundation.** If you're leaning toward STEM, take Calculus 1. If you're leaning toward the humanities or social sciences, take Introduction to Statistics. If you have absolutely no idea yet, statistics is the more versatile choice — it's used in business, psychology, social science, and natural science alike.

If you start with exactly 15 credits and nothing more, you leave yourself no room to maneuver.

## 🍽️ 3. Field-by-Field Tasting Menu

Pick **3 fields that catch your eye** from the options below. Every course listed is based on actual Spring 2026 offering data, and all are introductory-level courses (first digit of course code = 1). Unless you're already confident in a particular major, avoid touching advanced courses.

### ⚙️ Engineering & Technology Sampler

Most introductory courses in this field are 2 credits, making them low-commitment. Think of them as a minimum investment to answer the question: "Is engineering right for me?"

**Introduction to Software (GCS10001)** — 2 credits, 6 sections (분반) offered

A course that teaches foundational programming concepts and logical thinking. Having zero prior coding experience is perfectly fine — in fact, this course is designed specifically for people who know nothing about coding. You'll start from the basics — variables, conditionals, loops — and build simple programs. It's the first step in determining whether programming fits your aptitude, and a course that builds essential digital-era competency regardless of your eventual major.

**Introduction to AI, Computer & Electronic Engineering (ECE10006)** — 2 credits, Mon periods 6–7

An introductory course that lets you sample three fields — AI, computer science, and electronic engineering — in a single course. It gives a broad overview of what problems each field tackles and what practitioners actually do. The emphasis is on breadth rather than depth, making it especially well-suited for students who think, "I have absolutely no sense of what engineering even is."

**Introduction to Front-End Development (GCS10081)** — 3 credits, 6 sections offered

A hands-on course where you build actual websites. You'll learn HTML, CSS, and JavaScript and produce visible results immediately — that's the appeal of this course. While Introduction to Software focuses on logical thinking, Introduction to Front-End focuses on **the joy of building things.** If you have any interest in design, this course lets you experience the intersection of technology and design.

**Introduction to Physics (GEK10090)** — 3 credits, Tue period 2 & Fri period 2 / Tue period 3 & Fri period 3

A course covering core physics concepts at a level accessible to non-majors. It's broader in scope but shallower in depth than Physics 1, making it ideal for students who think, "I want to understand what physics is about, but I'm not planning to major in it." It helps build engineering intuition, and even if you later choose a STEM major, it serves as solid foundational fitness.

### 📖 Humanities & Social Sciences Sampler

Most courses in this field are 3 credits, and many offer sections taught entirely in English. If English-medium instruction feels intimidating, simply choose a Korean-medium section.

**Introduction to Business Administration (MEC10002)** — 3 credits, 3 sections (**Section 01: 100% English**)

A course about how businesses work. In one semester, you'll survey the major domains of business: marketing, finance, human resources, and strategy. Whether you're interested in startups or corporate employment, understanding how organizations operate is useful in any career path. If there's even a slight chance you might start a business after graduation, I highly recommend taking this course.

**Introduction to Economics (MEC10001)** — 3 credits, 2 sections (**Section 01: 100% English**)

The study of how money and resources flow through the world. You'll learn about supply and demand, market principles, and the effects of government policy. After this course, you'll be able to answer questions like "Why does the news say interest rate hikes matter?" and "How does a rising exchange rate affect my daily life?" Because economics requires mathematical thinking, it serves as an excellent bridge for STEM-oriented students who want to explore a humanities/social science field.

**Introduction to Psychology (CSW10003)** — 3 credits, 3 sections (**Section 02: 100% English**)

The scientific study of the human mind and behavior. It covers core topics for understanding humans: perception, memory, learning, motivation, emotion, personality, and social behavior. If you're drawn to the question "Why do people behave the way they do?", this course is for you. Psychology uses a surprising amount of statistics and experimental design, making it a unique field that satisfies both scientific thinking and humanistic curiosity simultaneously.

**Introduction to International Relations (ISE10052)** — 3 credits, 2 sections (**Section 01: 100% English**)

A course about relations between nations and global issues. Topics include war and peace, trade, diplomacy, international organizations, and human rights. It's valuable not only for aspiring diplomats, international organization staff, or NGO workers, but for any student curious about "how the world works" — it will broaden your perspective considerably.

**Introduction to Philosophy (GEK10030)** — 3 credits, 2 sections (**Section 01: 100% English**)

"Why do I exist?" "What is justice?" "How can we know the truth?" This course tackles life's most fundamental questions. It may seem impractical, but philosophical thinking is the foundation of logical writing, critical analysis, and ethical judgment — skills essential to every field. Philosophy also connects deeply to AI ethics, law, and policy studies.

**Introduction to Sociology (GEK10040)** — 3 credits, Tue periods 2–3

A discipline that analyzes social structures and phenomena. It covers class, inequality, culture, institutions, and collective behavior, providing a systematic framework for thinking about "Why does society function this way?" Recommended for students who want to learn to examine not just individuals, but society as a whole.

**Introduction to Education (GEK20042)** — 3 credits, 4 sections offered

A course exploring the essence and principles of education. It's meaningful not only for aspiring teachers, but for anyone interested in teaching and developing people. The applications of education studies are broader than you might expect: corporate training, educational technology, educational content creation, and more.

### 🎨 Arts & Design Sampler

Arts and design courses tend to be practice-heavy, so the time investment can be greater than other courses. However, they offer a valuable opportunity to discover whether you have artistic sensibility.

**Drawing Fundamentals 1 (IID10004)** — 3 credits, Fri periods 2–3 / Fri periods 5–6 / Fri periods 7–8

A course in foundational drawing and visual expression. If you think "I can't draw" — don't worry. This course trains observation skills and expression techniques, not innate talent. It's a great starting point for anyone considering design, architecture, or media. A nice bonus: classes are only on Fridays, so it doesn't affect your schedule on other days.

**Introduction to Spatial Studies (CUE10004)** — 3 credits, Tue period 1 & Fri period 1 / Mon period 4 & Thu period 4

A foundational course in architecture and spatial design. It explores the principles behind how the spaces we inhabit are designed, and how space influences human behavior and emotion. If you have even a passing interest in architecture or spatial environment studies, I recommend using this course to get a feel for the field.

### 🏛️ History & Culture Sampler

History and culture courses broaden the lens through which you see the world. They hold high value as liberal arts regardless of your eventual major, and they train both writing ability and critical thinking simultaneously.

**History and Culture of the East (GEK10087)** — 3 credits, Mon period 3 & Thu period 3, **100% English**

A course covering the arc of East Asian civilization, centered on China, Japan, and Korea. Since it's taught entirely in English, you'll improve your language skills as well. It provides excellent background knowledge for students interested in international relations, cultural content, or diplomacy.

**History and Culture of the West (GEK10088)** — 3 credits, Mon periods 2–3 / Mon periods 5–6

A course tracing the development of Western civilization — from ancient Greece and Rome through modern democratic revolutions and the Industrial Revolution. It's essential background for understanding why today's global political, economic, and social structures look the way they do. Both sections run as back-to-back classes (2 consecutive hours) on Mondays, meaning you can concentrate your lectures on Monday and keep the rest of the week free for other courses.

**Understanding Korean Literature (GEK10033)** — 3 credits, Thu periods 6–7

A course in reading and analyzing Korean literary works across genres: novels, poetry, and essays. It develops literary sensitivity and critical reading skills. It also provides a solid foundation for students interested in content creation, media, or publishing.

**Globalization and Korean Popular Culture (GEK10104)** — 3 credits, Tue period 2 & Fri period 2, **100% English**

An academic analysis of how K-pop, K-drama, K-food, and other Korean popular culture went global. Taught entirely in English, it explores the relationship between cultural content and globalization. It's an engaging topic that connects not only to the "Korean Wave" but also to media, marketing, international relations, and more.

## ⚠️ 5. Things to Watch Out for While Exploring

### 🎯 Distinguish "Fun" from "Aptitude"

Just because the first class was enjoyable doesn't necessarily mean the field is your calling. Most professors craft their opening lectures to be engaging and appealing. True aptitude is measured by **whether you still want to keep going when you hit the hard parts.**

For example, if the first business class was fun during the case study analysis but your interest vanished the moment accounting numbers appeared, you may have been attracted only to the glamorous surface of business. Conversely, if working with accounting numbers is difficult but you keep wondering "Why is this processed this way?" — that persistent curiosity is the signal of genuine aptitude.

### 👥 Leverage Senior Student (섬김이) Interviews

The real information isn't in the course catalog — it's with your seniors. I recommend meeting at least one upperclassman from each major and asking these three questions:

- "What's the hardest part of this major?"
- "What are you doing after graduation? / What do you plan to do?"
- "If you were a freshman again, would you choose this major?"

These three questions give you more information than the entire course catalog. The answer to the third question, in particular, tends to be the most honest and the most useful.

> Note: At Handong, 섬김이 (seomgimi) are student mentors assigned to freshman groups. They are excellent first contacts for this kind of candid conversation.

### 🏷️ Check the Course Codes

The first digit of a course code indicates the target year level. Courses with 1 are for first-years; those with 3 or 4 are for third- and fourth-years. Unless you've firmly decided on a major, **sticking to first-year introductory courses is the safe play.** Upper-level courses often have prerequisites (courses you must take first), and taking them without background knowledge just results in a damaged GPA.

### ⏳ Postponing a Decision Is Still a Decision

Exploration is important, but **you cannot explore forever.** Spend the first semester gaining experience, then start setting a direction in the second semester. The perfect choice does not exist in this world. When you reach 70% certainty, move. The remaining 30% fills itself in through action. Those who wait for the perfect moment end up never starting anything at all.

## 🔭 6. Second Semester Strategy

Depending on your first-semester exploration results, three paths open up.

**If you've found a direction**, switch to the relevant detailed guide. If you're leaning toward engineering, AI, or natural sciences, consult the [[STEM Freshman Guide]]. If you're drawn to business, economics, law, or international relations, consult the [[Humanities & Social Science Guide]]. Start taking core major foundation courses from your second semester onward.

**If you're still unsure**, add 1–2 introductory courses from fields you haven't tried yet, while simultaneously beginning foundation courses in whichever field attracted you most during the first semester. For example, if economics was the most interesting, take one more economics foundation course in the second semester while adding an untried arts/design introductory course.

**If two fields are pulling you equally**, take advantage of Handong's double major or interdisciplinary major systems. It's entirely feasible. For instance, if both computer science and business excite you, you can pursue one as your primary major and the other as a double major. You can also design a custom converged major through GCS that fuses both fields.
